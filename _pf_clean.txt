A Modular Framework for LLM-Guided Pattern Analysis
Anonymous Author(s)
ABSTRACT
Exploratory pattern analysis is difficult not simply because data are
large, but because end-to-end analytical workflows require coordi-
nated, strategic reasoning over textual and tabular data alike. In this
work, we present a modular framework for LLM-assisted pattern
analysis. Our framework formulates inputs to language models
in the forms of triples containing (a) mathematical definitions, (b)
corresponding textual interpretation, and (c) a set of example pairs
gluing textual and tabular evidence. Based on these, we map users
from partially specified pattern components to target components,
thus enabling pattern recognition, formulation, as well as instance
discovery to be expressed within a unified formal representation.
We investigate whether such complementary representations of
pattern knowledge, including examples, textual descriptions, formal
definitions, and their combinations, improve LLM-guided explo-
ration in an end-to-end, analyst-like workflow environment. We
instantiate the approach through a suite of prompting strategies
and evaluate our proof-of-concept over the USPTO dataset and its
corresponding WIPO report corpus. Our results validate that modu-
lar, component-wise grounding can help make LLM reasoning more
coherent in exploratory pattern analysis, while also highlighting
the current limits of LLMs for the same.

1 INTRODUCTION
Pattern analysis using large language models (LLMs) is challenging
not only because data are large, but because meaningful patterns
must often be articulated before they can be searched, compared,
or validated. For non-experts, the barrier may be partially technical
and partly conceptual: even when an interesting phenomenon is
visible, it may not be intuitive how to formalize that observation
or tie it across multiple sources of evidence. Recent advancements
in language model capabilities have created new opportunities to
lower this barrier by supporting more natural interaction with
data. However, as the complexity associated with underlying tasks
increases, purely linguistic prompting can become insufficient for

Our problem is inherently evident in exploratory workflows that
comprise a mixture of structured and unstructured data. For ex-
ample, a textual report may describe an interesting pattern - for
instance, a major turning point, convergence across trends, or asso-
ciation rules - while the corresponding evidence may live in struc-
tured counts or time series. In order to facilitate powerful human-AI
workflows, LLM-powered agents must therefore do more than an-
swer questions fluently: they mustrecognizecross-modal evidence,
formulate the mathematical logicof a pattern, and further use that
logic tosearch for insightsin an integrated manner.
In this paper, we address this problem by proposing a modular
framework for LLM-assisted pattern analysis. To this light, we
represent a pattern in the form of pattern triple P=(M,T,E)
as a composition of mathematical definition (M), its textual in-
terpretation (T ) and examples (E). By formulating analysis as a
mapping from partially known components to desired components,
our proposed framework leverages the concept of component-wise
grounding: the model resolves one analytical element at a time
instead of collapsing the objective into a single hop. This design
is intended to both reduce contextual drift and keep reasoning
anchored to the available evidence.
The core focus of our work is to investigate the aforesaid design
principle in contrast to claiming a general-purpose discovery en-
gine. Our focus is to test whether complementary representations
of pattern knowledge - including examples, textual descriptions,
formal definitions, as well as their combinations - materially im-
prove an LLM’s ability to recognize, formalize, and operationalize
patterns in an end-to-end exploratory workflow. In this paper, we
investigate and test our hypothesis through a USPTO case study
using structured, tabular data and a corresponding WIPO report
corpus. Our key contributions include:
[leftmargin=0pt]
• We re-imagine and formalize the pattern-analysis work-
flows as transformations over multiple key modular pattern
components.
• We propose a framework that supports component-wise
grounding through mathematical definitions, textual de-
scriptions, and example pairs.
• We perform an extensive performance evaluation study on
the USPTO and WIPO datasets demonstrating how different
pattern representations affect LLM-guided exploration in
practice.

3 FRAMEWORK
The goal of this framework is to formulate analytical tasks by mod-
ularizingpatternsanddatainto components. These components act
as building blocks that allow analytical problems to be expressed
in a structured format to the computational tools.
Instead of defining broad research goals, the framework encourages
users to express analytical tasks as mappings between patterns as
InputsandOutputs. Users take the information currently available,
such as mathematical definitions of multiple patterns, examples of
a single pattern, etc., and define it as the input to a tool. They then
specify a desired output, which may represent new knowledge that
is not currently available to them. The transformation from input
to output is performed by the LLM. The input–output formulation
allows users to clearly state what information is given to the tool
and what result they expect in return.
3.1 Pattern and Data Representation
The framework operates on patterns as primary objects of analysis,
with data providing the source from which patterns are identified
and the context in which they are grounded.
This framework works with two types of data:tabular data(𝐷.𝑡𝑎𝑏 )
andtextual data( 𝐷.𝑡𝑒𝑥𝑡 ). Tabular data refers to structured datasets
such as spreadsheets or relational tables. Textual data refers to
information expressed in natural language, such as patent landscape
reports, corporate reports, or technical documents. For the purpose
of this study, tabular data corresponds to asingle unified table,
and reports correspond to aset of textual reports.
A Pattern is defined as a recurring structural behavior found in
data. For instance, in the context of multiple time-series datasets,
one can observe periods where the gap between series either closes
(convergence) or widens (divergence). The behavior portrayed by
data during these periods can be called a convergence or divergence
pattern. Similarly, all such observable structural behaviors, where
data follows a recognizable and repeatable logic, we specify them
as patterns.
To facilitate analysis, a pattern is modularized into these compo-
nents: a Mathematical Definition (𝑀), a Textual Description (𝑇 ),
and a set of Examples (𝐸).

4 METHODOLOGY
Our methodology is organized as a case study designed to investi-
gate the framework presented in Section 3 along three complemen-
tary dimensions. Rather than positioning the framework against a
4
===== PAGE 5 =====
predetermined baseline or claiming a general-purpose reasoning
advantage, we examine whether the framework’s modular repre-
sentation (a) changes the reliability of LLM outputs when it is made
available in the prompt, (b) exposes a structured landscape of prob-
lem difficulty that is interpretable through its own components,
and (c) treats its three components (𝑀,𝑇 ,𝐸) as functionally distinct
carriers of pattern knowledge rather than redundant encodings.
The three research questions are investigated through two com-
plementary experimental structures. RQ1 is addressed through a
paired comparison between the framework and a non-framework
prose condition on selected problem formulations from the catalog.
RQ2 and RQ3 are addressed jointly through a subtractive ablation
grid run entirely within the framework condition, in which input
components are progressively removed while the target is held
fixed. The two structures are connected: the ablation grid opera-
tionalizes a family of problem formulations within the framework’s
notation, and its results inform both the difficulty landscape of RQ2
and the component-distinctness analysis of RQ3.
4.1 Case Study Setting
We instantiate our study over a bounded subset of the USPTO patent
dataset and a curated corpus of WIPO patent landscape reports.
From the USPTO dataset we construct pre-aggregated tabular slices
scoped to the technology areas covered by the reports, at an aggre-
gation granularity (year× field× country, with US-centric filtering)
that matches the level at which the reports discuss trends. From
each report we manually extract a small set of pattern instances,
hand-authoring their components as gold references. The resulting
case study operates over a compact and verifiable data environment
where both tabular and textual evidence for each pattern can be
directly inspected.
Each pattern instance is represented according to the notation in-
troduced in Section 3. Each analytical problem is specified as a
configuration of given components, absent components, and target
components, following the “?” and “_” conventions defined in the
framework. This notation is the sole interface through which prob-
lems are posed to the LLM in the framework-enabled conditions of
our study.

4.4 Research Questions and Experimental
Design
We structure our study around three research questions. RQ1 is a
comparativequestion that contrasts the framework against a non-
framework prose alternative. RQ2 and RQ3 arewithin-framework
questions that characterize the framework’s own internal structure.
They are therefore evaluated under different experimental designs:
RQ1 uses a paired framework-vs-prose comparison on the selected
formulations, while RQ2 and RQ3 share a single subtractive ablation
grid defined in Section 4.5.
4.4.1 RQ1: Does the availability of the framework’s modular rep-
resentation change the reliability of LLM outputs?We test whether
exposing the LLM to the framework’s component vocabulary and
notation leads to more reliable outputs than presenting the same
information in unstructured prose. We hold the information con-
tent constant across conditions and vary only the representational
scaffolding available to the model. In both conditions, reasoning is
enabled and the model is free to organize its reasoning as it sees fit;
we do not prescribe a decomposition strategy in either condition.
Framework condition.The prompt introduces the framework’s no-
tation and component vocabulary (𝑀,𝑇 ,𝐸, with 𝐸 decomposed
into(𝐸text,𝐸 tab) pairs), specifies the problem in the framework’s
notation, and provides the given components in their labeled form.
The LLM is expected to produce the target component(s) using the
framework’s vocabulary.
Non-framework condition.The prompt presents the same informa-
tion in prose without component labels, notation, or structural com-
mitments. Pattern information is woven into a natural-language
narrative, and the task is described as a question or instruction in
plain language.
Both conditions are templatized, with the underlying pattern con-
tent held identical. We avoid leaking framework-specific structural
commitments (such as the triple structure, the pair structure of
examples, or the correspondence between𝐸text and𝐸tab) into the
non-framework prompt. Conversely, no framework-specific instruc-
tion to decompose is added; any decomposition behavior that arises
is attributed to the LLM’s own reasoning. Each selected formula-
tion from Section 4.2 is run under both conditions, yielding a set of
paired runs per pattern instance.
We measure reliability along two axes: output agreement with gold
references, and output consistency across models when given the
same prompt. We additionally inspect the LLM’s reasoning traces
(as produced by reasoning-enabled decoding) and manually label a
stratified sample for evidence of component-wise decomposition.
This allows us to observe whether the framework, when made
available, induces structured reasoning behavior that differs from
what the LLM does spontaneously under the prose condition.
4.4.2 RQ2: Does the framework’s modular representation expose a
structured landscape of problem difficulty?We test whether perfor-
mance across different input–target configurations, when viewed

through the framework’s lens, reveals interpretable structure rather
than arbitrary variation. If the framework’s decomposition into𝑀,
𝑇 , and𝐸 carries meaningful semantic content, we expect that (a)
the difficulty of recovering different targets varies systematically,
(b) this variation correlates with framework-level properties of
the problem (e.g., which component is being produced, whether

RQ2 is answered entirely from the ablation grid described in Sec-

dimensions: target component type, the amount of information
available to the model, and the modality of the remaining inputs
relative to the target.
4.4.3 RQ3: Are the framework’s three components functionally dis-
tinct?We test whether the components 𝑀,𝑇 , and𝐸 carry meaning-
fully different information by holding the target fixed and systemat-
ically varying which other components are available as input. If the
three components were redundant encodings of the same under-
lying pattern knowledge, we would expect the choice of provided
input components to have little effect on output quality. If they are
functionally distinct, the LLM’s ability to produce the target should
depend systematically on which components are available, with
different removals producing different accuracy drops.
Like RQ2, RQ3 is answered from the ablation grid described in
Section 4.5. For each fixed target, we read the contribution profile
of single and paired removals. We additionally examine whether
different remaining input subsets lead to qualitatively different
kinds of outputs — for instance, whether outputs produced in the
presence of 𝑀 tend to be more structurally faithful while those
produced in the presence of𝑇 tend to be more narratively coherent.
4.5 Ablation Grid for RQ2 and RQ3
RQ2 and RQ3 are both within-framework questions: they charac-
terize how the framework behaves, independent of any comparison
to a non-framework alternative. We answer both through a single
subtractive ablation grid, run entirely under the framework con-
dition. This subsection describes the grid’s structure and the task
hierarchy it encodes.
Grid structure.The grid is a table with four columns — one per
atomic target component (𝑀,𝑇 ,𝐸text,𝐸tab) — and nine rows in-
dicating which components are removed from the input relative
to a full-inputs baseline. Each cell corresponds to one analytical
task expressible in the framework’s notation: produce the column’s
target, given all components of the pattern except those specified
in the row. The nine rows are (i) the full-inputs baseline with no
removals, (ii) four single-component removals ({𝑀} ,{𝑇} ,{𝐸text},
{𝐸tab}), and (iii) four selected two-component removals ({𝑀,𝑇} ,
{𝐸text,𝐸 tab},{𝑀,𝐸 tab},{𝑇,𝐸 text}). Cells in which the removed set
contains the target are undefined and left blank; the remaining cells
constitute the meaningful conditions under study.
Subtractive framing.We adopt a subtractive rather than additive
design because it measures themarginal contributionof each com-
ponent in the presence of the others, which we view as the most
informative regime for characterizing functional distinctness. An
additive counterpart, starting from empty inputs and progressively
adding components, would characterize standalone sufficiency in-
stead; this is a complementary question that we leave to future
work.
Task hierarchy.The nine rows form a shallow hierarchy organized
by how much information is withheld from the model. The baseline
row (no removal) corresponds to the maximum-context tasks, in
which the model receives every component of the pattern other
than the target. The four single-removal rows correspond to tasks
in which exactly one component is withheld, and together they
isolate the marginal contribution of each individual component to
each target. The four double-removal rows correspond to tasks in
which two related components are withheld simultaneously, and
are chosen to probe specific structural claims of the framework:
(i){𝑀,𝑇} withholds both definitional forms of the pattern, testing
whether the mathematical and textual definitions are mutually sub-
stitutable or jointly necessary; (ii){𝐸text,𝐸 tab} withholds the entire
example set, testing whether the pair structure contributes beyond
either example alone; and (iii){𝑀,𝐸 tab} and{𝑇,𝐸 text} withhold one
definitional and one exemplar component, testing cross-kind com-
plementarity between definitions and examples. We deliberately
do not enumerate all 4

Connection to the problem catalog.Each cell of the grid instantiates
a problem expressible in the notation of Section 3. For instance, the
cell at (row:{𝑀} removed, column:𝐸text) corresponds to a problem

its paired tabular example. In this sense the grid is a systematic

pattern single-target configurations for tractability. Cross-pattern
formulations, which are also part of the framework’s catalog, are
not included in the grid and are left for future work or qualitative
treatment.
Mapping to RQ2 and RQ3.RQ2 reads across the grid. The full-inputs
baseline row gives the ceiling accuracy for each target, indicat-

maximal context. The degradation pattern — how accuracy falls as
components are removed — characterizes each target’s sensitivity
to input composition. Together these form the difficulty landscape
of the framework’s problem space.
RQ3 reads within each column. Comparing the drops produced
by different single-component removals for the same target tests
whether those removals carry equivalent information (components
are redundant) or differentiated information (components are func-
tionally distinct). The double-removal rows further decompose the
picture: sub-additive drops indicate that the two removed com-
ponents were partially substitutable, while super-additive drops
indicate joint necessity.

4.7 Evaluation Protocol

nation of automated checks, LLM-as-judge scoring, and limited
manual inspection. Schema validity of produced𝑀 is checked au-

LIMITATIONS
The pattern analysis framework presented in this paper seeks to
benefit from human-in-the-loop methodologies (which this paper
defers to future efforts). The performance of our framework is in-
herently reliant on the ground-data provided to the language model,
which is in-turn determined by underlying pattern-extraction and
text-extraction tools. It is further constrained by the reasoning ca-
pabilities of the embedded LLM, which our framework considers
as black-box. Further note, that our solution is only as good as
the quality of both tabular as well as textual data provided by an
end-user (garbage-in garbage-out). We leave deeper investigations
into these problems to domain experts and/or to future research.
