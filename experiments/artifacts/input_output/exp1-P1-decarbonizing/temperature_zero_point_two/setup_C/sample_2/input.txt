With approximately 94% of this\nsector currently powered by fossil fuels, decarbonization represents a crucial opportunity for\nemissions reduction.\nThis patent landscape report examines innovation trends in those technologies that are\nenabling the decarbonization of heavy-duty road transport, analyzing patent data from 2000\nto 2024 across four core technology areas: low-emission energy sources (batteries, hydrogen\nfuel cells, hybrids, and alternative fuels), energy infrastructure, vehicle efficiency, and\ngreen digitalization.\nThe innovation landscape has undergone a fundamental transformation. The share of patents\nrelated to decarbonization technologies increased from around 7% of all heavy-duty road\ntransport patents in 2000 to approximately 20% in 2024, with published patent families growing\nfrom around 1,200 to almost 15,400 annually. Electrification has emerged as the dominant\npathway, evidenced by a large volume of patenting activity and strong growth across the entire\nelectrification value chain. In 2024, batteries accounted for 73% of all low-emission energy\nsource patents, while charging infrastructure and smart grids have lower levels of patenting in\ncomparison, but have growth similar to what is being seen across other major technology areas.\nHydrogen technologies remain smaller in scale, but are gaining momentum, with patenting\nroughly doubling between 2019 and 2024. This suggests hydrogen could play a role in specific\nniches, particularly long-haul transport, though the technological and economic barriers remain\nsubstantial. Hybrid vehicle patenting has plateaued, increasingly overshadowed by fully electric\nsolutions, while alternative low-carbon fuels show limited innovation activity, indicating they will\nlikely remain niche or transitional solutions.\nPatenting activity is geographically concentrated. China and the United States lead in absolute\npatent volumes. In China, annual patent publications have risen from just 11 in 2000 to around\n7,300 in 2024, though Chinese inventors focus primarily on domestic patent protection. India\nhas recorded even higher growth rates over recent years, albeit from a lower baseline, driven by\ngovernment initiatives supporting electric bus deployment. Sweden and Germany demonstrate\nexceptionally high relative specialization across multiple decarbonization fields, reflecting their\nstrong truck manufacturing industries.\nLarge automotive manufacturers and suppliers dominate innovation. Toyota leads patent\nrankings across all technology areas, followed by Volkswagen (including its subsidiary Traton,\nwith the Scania and MAN brands), Hyundai, Ford, and major suppliers including Bosch and ZF.\nNotably, no universities or public research institutions appear among the top patent holders,\nunderscoring the mature, industry-driven nature of heavy-duty road transport innovation.\nThe patent landscape reveals that technological foundations for decarbonizing heavy-duty road\ntransport are advancing rapidly, with battery-electric solutions clearly dominant. A slowdown\nor plateauing of patenting activity was observed in 2024 across most technology areas, though\nwhether this represents a temporary fluctuation or a longer-term shift remains to be seen.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 7 / 100 =====\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Decarbonizing Heavy-Duty Road Transport\n\n8\b\n\nInnovation activity is notably concentrated among major automotive players in a handful of\ncountries, a pattern consistent with sector maturity. These\ntechnologies differ significantly in their maturity, infrastructure requirements, and suitability\nfor specific use cases, but together they define the main options for reducing tailpipe emissions\nin heavy-duty vehicles.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 13 / 100 =====\n\n14\b\n\nComplementary to low-emission vehicles is the need for a robust, scalable energy infrastructure\ncapable of supporting widespread deployment. The operational feasibility of low-emission\nbuses and trucks depends on the availability and performance of energy distribution networks,\nincluding high-power charging systems, smart grids, as well as hydrogen production, storage\nand refueling infrastructure.\nImprovements in vehicle efficiency remain a crucial supporting strategy for decarbonizing\nheavy-duty road transport, irrespective of the underlying energy source. Efficiency gains\ndirectly reduce the amount of energy required to transport goods and passengers, thereby\nlowering operating costs and lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions. Relevant technologies include\nadvances in electric powertrains and drivetrains, vehicle aerodynamics, lightweight and highperformance materials, and reductions in tire rolling resistance.\nFinally, digital technologies and data-driven fleet optimization offer additional opportunities to\nreduce emissions through improved vehicle operation and system-level efficiency. Applications\nsuch as platooning, smart routing, and vehicle-to-infrastructure communication can reduce\nenergy consumption by minimizing idle time, smoothing driving patterns, anticipating charging\nor refueling needs, and improving asset utilization. While these technologies do not replace\nthe need for low-emission energy sources, they play an important complementary role by\noptimizing the energy use across heavy-duty vehicle fleets.\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Decarbonizing Heavy-Duty Road Transport\n\nTogether, these four technology areas form the analytical framework for the patent-based\nassessment presented in the following chapters, which examine innovation trends, leading\nactors, and the geographical patterns shaping the decarbonization of buses and heavyduty trucks.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 14 / 100 =====\n\n2 Decarbonizing heavyduty road transport:\nglobal patent trends\nGlobal patenting trends in technologies supporting the decarbonization of heavy-duty road\ntransport are examined in this chapter. TCO is calculated by adding together all the costs involved in acquiring,\noperating, maintaining and disposing of a vehicle over its useful life, and then expressing these\ncosts on a per-kilometer or per-year basis. A TCO analysis helps businesses optimize costs and\nmake informed decisions about fleet management.\nHowever, because heavy-duty vehicles operate in different duty cycles, TCO results are\nvocation dependent. It is also vital to establish a robust, scalable energy infrastructure backbone.\nThe deployment and operational efficiency of zero-emission trucks and buses is fundamentally\nconstrained by the availability of energy distribution networks. This infrastructure includes\nhigh-power charging networks for battery-electric vehicles (BEVs) and grid updates, as well as\nhydrogen generation, storage, distribution, and refueling stations for fuel cell vehicles (FCEVs).\nThis chapter explores each of these domains and the technical and economic hurdles driving\nsignificant global patenting activity.\n–\n\n–\n\n–\n\n–\n\nBattery-electric heavy-duty vehicles (HDVs) require a widespread and reliable charging\ninfrastructure, ranging from private depot chargers to minimize operating costs, to public\nhigh-power stations located along freight transport routes to maintain capacity. Current\ninnovation in this sector is focused on overcoming the time constraints of recharging large\nbattery packs, with the aim of enabling charging times comparable to driver rest breaks.\nHowever, the shift to megawatt-scale power transfer introduces significant technical\nchallenges. They include the thermal management of cables and connectors (e.g., liquid\ncooling), standardizing interoperable interfaces and integrating stations into the power grid.\nDespite the rapid expansion of heavy-duty charging networks in China, Europe and\nNorthern America in recent years, infrastructure deployment lags behind the trajectory\nrequired for large-scale fleet electrification. Patent analysis highlights strong innovation growth led by\npowertrain technologies, identifies key inventor locations and companies, and shows\nhow vehicle efficiency improvements support the broader decarbonization of heavy-duty\nroad transport.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 55 / 100 =====\n\n56\b\n\nThe drive for greater efficiency\nMaximizing vehicle efficiency is a critical, complementary strategy for achieving comprehensive\ndecarbonization of heavy-duty road transport. Efficiency improvements are essential regardless\nof the energy source (electric, hydrogen, low-carbon fuel-based or hybrid) because they directly\nreduce the total energy required to transport goods, thereby lowering both operational costs\nand overall life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions. In the context of new energy vehicles like\nbattery-electric vehicles and fuel cell electric vehicles, higher vehicle efficiency translates\ndirectly into a smaller, lighter and less expensive battery or hydrogen storage system, or\nan extended driving range for a given energy capacity. For vehicles using low-carbon fuels,\nefficiency minimizes the demand for these often costly and supply-constrained resources.\nThe drive for greater efficiency in heavy-duty road transport centers on the three main\ntechnological pillars detailed below:\n–\n\n–\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Decarbonizing Heavy-Duty Road Transport\n\n–\n\nElectric power- and drivetrains represent a fundamental shift from mechanical to electrical\npropulsion, requiring distinct optimizations compared to traditional combustion engines.\nThis category includes the development of e-axles, including electric motors and advanced\npower electronics, such as inverters that manage energy flow with minimal loss. It also\ncovers specialized gearing for electric vehicles, such as multi-speed transmissions designed\nto keep electric motors within their optimal efficiency range during the diverse load and\nspeed conditions typical of heavy-duty road transport. While electric motors are inherently\nefficient, innovations are essential to maximize regenerative braking capability and manage\nthe thermal challenges of high-power continuous operation. Ongoing progress in inverter\ndesign, thermal management and power electronics helps to reduce energy losses, extend\nvehicle range and improve performance in demanding duty cycles.\nVehicle design focuses on minimizing the external forces that resist vehicle\nmovement: aerodynamic drag and rolling resistance. As battery-electric and fuel-cell\ntrucks often carry limited onboard energy compared to diesel tanks, reducing energy\nwaste is paramount. Innovations in this field range from streamlined cab shapes and\nmirror-replacement camera systems to drag-reducing trailer add-ons. Furthermore, the\ndevelopment of specialized tires is critical; these must balance low rolling resistance to\nextend range with the durability required to support the increased weight of battery\npacks and the permanent solicitation of electric motors, which are constantly giving or\nrecovering energy.\nThe use of advanced materials addresses the challenge of “lightweighting” buses and\ntrucks to offset the substantial mass of zero-emission energy storage systems. Heavy\nbattery packs can significantly reduce a truck’s commercial payload capacity, directly\nimpacting revenue. To update this technology, the industry is researching and developing\nvarious lighter-weight materials from high-strength steel to cast aluminum and highperformance materials such as carbon fiber reinforced polymers and ceramics (often used\nin bearings or thermal management components). These materials offer high strength-toweight ratios that allow for structural weight reduction without compromising safety or\ndurability. However, widespread adoption of these materials faces challenges due to high\nmanufacturing costs.\n\nTogether, these technological areas form the foundation of a comprehensive efficiency strategy\nfor heavy-duty road transport. Improvements in these technologies reduce total energy\ndemand, lower operating costs, enable smaller batteries or hydrogen storage systems, and\nultimately enhance the feasibility and economics of low-emission freight and bus operations.\nAs the sector transitions toward electrification and new vehicle architectures, improvements\nin drivetrain efficiency, optimized design and advanced materials will become even more\ncritical to meeting climate targets while maintaining performance and competitiveness. This\nchapter explores the patent landscape across these three intertwined domains, detailing how\ntechnological innovation in efficiency is accelerating the practical and economic viability of\ndecarbonized heavy-duty road transport solutions.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 56 / 100 =====\n\n\b\n\nPatent spotlight\n\n57\n\nA Michelin patent application describes a truck tire with a stiff outer bead design that reduces\nbending hot spots, improves heat resistance and provides enhanced casing durability.\n–\n–\n–\n–\n\nPatent publication number: WO 2021/126187 A1\nOwner: Michelin\nTitle: Truck tire with stiff outer bead products\nPublication date: June 24, 2021\n\nPatent drawing\n\nSource: PATENTSCOPE (https://patentscope.wipo.int/\nsearch/en/detail.jsf?docId=WO2021126187).\n\nAI simulation\n\nSource: AI-generated by WIPO.\n\nProblem: Heavy truck tires are prone to bending hot spots and reduced heat resistance due\nto flexible bead core designs, which increase manufacturing complexity and cost and do not\neffectively eliminate bending issues.\nSolution: A bead design featuring two bead layers with stiffness of 8–14 MPa, positioned to\nreduce or eliminate bending hot spots and enhance heat resistance, while avoiding the use of\ncoextruded materials and complex chafer configurations.\nBenefit: The design effectively reduces bending hot spots, improves heat resistance, and\nenhances casing ply fatigue resistance without increasing manufacturing costs, by distributing\nhydrostatic stresses and providing rigidity to the tire’s casing ply.1\n\nGlobal patent development\nGlobal patent development in vehicle efficiency\nFrom 2000 to 2024, the volume of published patent families related to heavy-duty vehicle\nefficiency expanded substantially, growing from approximately 750 to more than 5,100\npublished families (Figure 5.1). Operators using trucks\nand buses to meet freight and passenger requirements face two major challenges: the\nimplementation of decarbonization solutions and driver shortages. Digitalization offers an\nopportunity to address both challenges, though it also poses a threat, since the industry is\nheavily paperwork-reliant worldwide. These pressures are pushing the road transport industry\nto revolutionize its operation in a shift away from the thoughtful evolution of incremental\noptimizations that has defined current practices. Road transport is the backbone of modern\neconomies, with any operational disruption representing a significant market risk.\nThe concepts that will define the adoption of sustainable trucks and buses to meet economic\nand client needs are the total cost of ownership and capacity. The major bottleneck is one\nrelated to the total cost of ownership (TCO) – namely, capacity pairing.\nWhile the TCO of zero-emission vehicles is sometimes lower than for diesel vehicles during first\nownership, their higher retail prices require companies to mobilize greater capital, thereby\ndelaying the transition. This is particularly difficult when freight rates are expected to remain\nconstant across powertrains, and interest and insurance rates remain high, and questions about\nzero-emission vehicle residual values create uncertainty for banks with regard to the financing\nof the transition.\nRegarding capacity, zero-emission vehicles can only operate if they have access to energy. Public\ninfrastructure remains too weak, is poorly adapted to heavy-goods vehicle dimensions and\nweights, and too expensive to create a favorable TCO. Private infrastructure is growing stronger,\nbut carriers still experience significant delays in setting up depot chargers and accessing a grid\nthat has adequate power. Moreover, private infrastructure is limited to operating the shorter\nrange of zero-emission vehicles, making long-range and international operations difficult\nwithout partnerships.\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Decarbonizing Heavy-Duty Road Transport\n\nThe crucial question to be answered is “Will there be a vehicle available at the right place\nand time, and at the right price?” The analysis conducted in this report demonstrates that\ntechnological innovations are gaining both traction and interest across several markets. Fossil\nfuel-powered vehicles have set a strong benchmark for zero- and low-carbon technologies\nto replicate and meet going forward. Global transport operators are evaluating the carbon\nreduction potential of greener energies with pragmatism.\nThis joint analysis by IRENA and WIPO shows that the decarbonization of heavy-duty vehicles\nwill leverage a combination of greener powertrains and greater operational efficiency. The most\nanticipated solutions focus on electrification, given the rapid advancements being made in this\narea. Biofuels, carbon-neutral fuels, and hydrogen are alternative decarbonization pathways,\nwith varying degrees of expectation. From an efficiency point of view, efforts are focused\non improving driver performance, improving fleet efficiency through more energy-efficient\nvehicles replacing older ones, and more efficient logistics to reduce the number of empty miles\nand increase vehicle occupancy rates. The adoption and integration of new digital technologies\ncan help in better understanding current operations before optimizing them, and in facilitating\ncommunication among multiple stakeholders. This deeper understanding will maximize the\npotential of existing technologies and practices to lower carbon emissions – the potential for\nefficiency-driven decarbonization is enormous.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 92 / 100 =====\n\nRecommendations\n\n\b\n\n93\n\nAccelerating sustainability efforts within the heavy-duty road transport sector is a conduit\nthat can greatly contribute toward the decarbonization of the global transport industry. The IP office will conduct an examination to\ndecide whether to grant or reject the application. Patent applications are generally published\n18 months after the earliest priority date of the application. Prior to that publication, the\napplication remains confidential.\n\nPatent classification\nPatent classification is a system that allows examiners of IP offices or other people to code\ndocuments, such as published patent applications, according to the technical features of their\ncontent. The International Patent Classification (IPC) is agreed internationally. The European\nPatent Office (EPO) and United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) launched a joint\nproject to create the Cooperative Patent Classification (CPC) in order to harmonize the patent\nclassifications systems between the two offices.\n\nPatent applicant/owner\nPatents are filed by an applicant, which can be an organization or a natural person. Applicants\nare not inventors, even if sometimes they are similar. The applicant is, in most jurisdictions and\nin most cases, published with the patent and always remains the applicant. The applicant is not\nautomatically the owner of a patent at a given time, even if that is often the case. Patents can\nbe transferred or sold, or the applicant itself can be sold as a company in a merger or takeover.\nTherefore, the “owner” of a patent might change over time and is not always published.\nFor proper analysis, to consolidate incorrect spelling and to include merger and acquisition\ninformation in the analysis, the report used the ultimate owner concept in the IFI Claims global\npatent database. The most probable entity was then named as owner.\n\nPatent family\nA patent family is a collection of patent applications covering the same or similar technical\ncontent and all sharing one or more priority documents. Families are used to count\ninventions, rather than several patents corresponding to the same subject matter filed in\ndifferent jurisdictions. There are several definitions of patent families, including simple and\nextended patent families, depending on the number of priority documents shared (ranging from\none to all priority documents). Patent family members are the individual patents filed in those\njurisdictions where a patent applicant is seeking patent protection (e.g., WIPO, EPO) and all\npublications in relation to these. In the present study, we counted simple patent families (using a\nrepresentative patent family member for each patent family), unless otherwise specified.\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Decarbonizing Heavy-Duty Road Transport\n\nGranted patent\nOnce examined by the IP office, an application becomes a granted patent or is rejected. If\ngranted, the patent gives its owner a temporary right, for a limited time period (normally\n20 years), to prevent unauthorized use of the technology outlined in the patent. The procedure\nfor granting patents varies widely between locations in accordance with national laws and\ninternational agreements.
