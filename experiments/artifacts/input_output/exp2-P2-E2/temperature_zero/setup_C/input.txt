It has been described by many, including Nvidia CEO JenHsun Huang, as an “iPhone moment” for GenAI. This is because the OpenAI platform has made\nit easier for all users to access advanced GenAI programs, particularly large language models\n(LLMs). These models have reached new levels of performance, demonstrating the potential\nfor various real-world applications, triggering a wave of research and development, and large\ncorporate investments in GenAI.\nThis WIPO Patent Landscape Report provides observations on patenting activity and scientific\npublications in the field of GenAI and builds on the 2019 WIPO Technology Trends publication on\nArtificial Intelligence. It aims to shed light on the current technology development, its changing\ndynamics and the applications in which GenAI technologies are expected to be used. It also\nidentifies key research countries, companies and organizations.\n\nGenAI patent families and scientific publications have increased\nsignificantly since 2017\nThe rise of GenAI over the past few years has been driven primarily by three factors: more\npowerful computers, the availability of large datasets as a source of training data, and improved\nAI/machine learning algorithms. Developments such as the transformer architecture in LLMs\nhave significantly advanced GenAI. This has made it possible to develop complex applications in\nmany different fields.\nThe technological advances in GenAI are reflected by the sharp increase in patenting activity.\nOver the past 10 years, the number of patent families in GenAI has grown from just only 733 in\n2014 to more than 14,000 in 2023. Since the introduction of the transformer in 2017, the deep\nneural network architecture behind the Large Language Models that have become synonymous\nwith GenAI, the number of GenAI patents has increased by over 800%. The number of scientific\npublications has increased even more over the same period, from just 116 in 2014 to more than\n34,000 in 2023. However, AI is not a new field of research. US and UK scientists – including\ntheoretical mathematician Alan Turing – were already working on machine learning in the\n1930s and 1940s, although the term AI did not become popular until the 1950s (McCarthy et al.\n2006).1 The 1950s and 1960s saw a surge of interest in many AI areas including natural language\nprocessing, machine learning and robotics. Some scientists at the time predicted that a machine\nas intelligent as a human would exist within a generation (Minsky 1967). These predictions\nproved to be overly optimistic. Progress stagnated because of the limitations of computing\npower and algorithmic approaches available at the time. As a result, research funding dried\nup, which led to the first “AI winter” in the 1970s. In the following decades, periods of high AI\nresearch intensity alternated with periods of lower activity.\nFor a long time, AI algorithms and software were developed for specific purposes, based on\nclear rules of logic and parameters specified by programmers. Even now, many AI applications\nrely on rule-based decisions: if this, then that. For example, virtual assistants (Siri, Alexa, etc.)\nare essentially command-and-control systems. They only understand a limited list of questions\nand requests and fail to adapt to new situations. They cannot apply their “knowledge” to new\nproblems or deal with uncertainty.\n\nAI in the 21st century\nThe modern AI boom started at the beginning of the 21st century and has been on an upward\ntrajectory ever since. Today, AI and machine learning is used in countless applications, including\nsearch engines, recommendation systems, targeted advertising, virtual assistants, autonomous\nvehicles, automatic language translation, facial recognition and many more. The rise of AI has\nbeen driven mainly by the following factors:\n–\n\n–\n\n–\n\n1\n2\n\nMore powerful computers: In 1965, Gordon Moore observed that the number of transistors\non computer chips doubles approximately every two years and predicted that this would\ncontinue for another 10 years (Moore 1965). His law has held true for more than half a\ncentury. This exponential growth translated into more and more powerful AI systems, often\nwith AI-specific enhancements.\nBig data: Second, the availability of data has increased similarly exponentially. This has\nprovided a powerful source of training data for AI algorithms and has made it possible to\ntrain models with billions of images or a hundred billion tokens2 of text.\nBetter AI/machine learning algorithms: New methods that allow AI systems to better use\ndata and algorithms to learn the way humans do, such as deep learning, have enabled\nbreakthroughs in areas such as image recognition or natural language processing\n(WIPO 2019).\n\nThe term “Artificial Intelligence” has been vastly influenced by John McCarthy at Dartmouth, who co-organized with\nMarvin Minsky the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence in 1956.\nTokens are common sequences of characters found in a set of text. Tokenization breaks text into smaller parts for\neasier machine analysis, helping AI models understand human language.\n\n\b13\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 16 / 116 =====\n\n14\b\n\nLearning with examples rather than rules\nThe heart of modern AI is machine learning, when computer systems learn without being\nspecifically programmed to do so. Modern AI models are fed with examples of input data\nand the desired outcome, allowing them to build models or programs that can be applied to\nentirely new data. Machine learning excels at handling massive datasets and uncovering hidden\npatterns within them.\nA powerful approach within machine learning is called deep learning. It leverages complex\nstructures called artificial neural networks, loosely modeled after the human brain. These\nnetworks identify patterns within datasets. The more data they have access to, the better they\nlearn and perform. Information flows through numerous layers of interconnected neurons,\nwhere it is processed and evaluated. Each layer refines the information, connecting and\nweighting it through nodes. Essentially, AI learns by continuously reassessing its knowledge,\nforming new connections and prioritizing information based on new data it encounters.\nThe term deep learning refers to the vast number of layers these networks can utilize. Deep\nlearning-powered AI has achieved remarkable advancements, especially in areas like image\nand speech recognition. However, its success comes with a drawback. While the accuracy of the\nresults is impressive, the decision-making process remains unclear, even to AI experts. This lack\nof transparency is a contrast to older rule-based systems.\n\nModern generative AI (GenAI): the next level of AI\nGenerative AI (GenAI) has been an active area of research for a long time. Joseph Weizenbaum\ndeveloped the very first chatbot, ELIZA, in the 1960s (Weizenbaum 1966). However, GenAI as we\nknow it today was heralded by the advent of deep learning based on neural networks.\nToday, GenAI is one of the most powerful examples of machine learning. Compared to old\nrule-based AI applications that could only perform a single task, modern GenAI models are\ntrained on data from many different areas, without any limitations in terms of task. Because\nthe amount of training data is so large – OpenAI’s GPT-3 was trained on more than 45 terabytes\nof compressed text data (Brown et al.2020) – the models appear to be creative in producing\noutputs. For example, traditional chatbots follow scripted responses and rely on pre-defined\nrules to interact with users, making them suitable only for specific tasks. In contrast, modern\nGenAI chatbots such as ChatGPT or Google Gemini can generate human-like text, allowing for\nconversations that can adapt to many topics without being confined to a predetermined script.\nIn addition, these modern chatbots can produce not only text, but also images, music and\ncomputer code based on the dataset on which they were trained.\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\nThe release of ChatGPT in 2022 was an iPhone moment for GenAI\nIn November 2022, OpenAI released ChatGPT (Chat Generative Pre-trained Transformer) to\nthe public, which greatly increased public enthusiasm for GenAI. More than one million people\nsigned up to use ChatGPT in just five days. A 2023 survey by auditing and consulting firm\nDeloitte found that nearly 61% of respondents in Switzerland who work with a computer already\nuse ChatGPT or other GenAI programs in their daily work (Deloitte 2023). The ChatGPT release\nhas been described by many, including Nvidia CEO Jen-Hsun Huang, as an “iPhone moment” for\nGenAI (VentureBeat 2023). This is partly because the platform made it easier for users to access\nadvanced GenAI models, specifically decoder-based large language models. 3 These models\nhave demonstrated the potential for many real-world applications and have sparked a wave of\nresearch and development. Many companies are heavily investing in GenAI, with these newer\nmodels reaching a new dimension of capabilities.\n\n3\n\nSee next chapter for an overview and description of the different modern GenAI models.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 17 / 116 =====\n\nYear\n\nEvent\n\n1957\n\n•\n\nFrank Rosenblatt introduces the perceptron, the fundamental building block of neural networks\n(Rosenblatt 1957)\n\n1972\n\n•\n\nAmari-Hopfield Networks make recurrent neural networks able to learn, as a form of associative\nmemory (Amari 1972, Hopfield 1982)\n\n1997\n\n•\n\nLong Short Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent neural networks are published, which will become one of\nthe most successful deep learning architectures in the 2010s (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber 1997)\n\n1990\n\n•\n\nMarkov networks and other statistical language models led to effective AI commercial systems, such\nas the first versions of Google Translate\n\n2013\n\n•\n\nVariational Autoencoders (VAEs), an Auto-encoder approach able to generate new realistic image\nsamples from input images (Kingma and Welling 2013)\n\n2014\n\n•\n\nGenerative Adversarial Networks are described, which will lead to various generative applications\naround photorealistic images\n\n2016\n\n•\n\nWaveNet by DeepMind, a novel deep neural network approach for realistic human speech (van den\nOord et al. 2016)\n\n2017\n\n•\n\nA team from Google Research introduces the transformer, the deep neural network architecture\nbehind the Large Language Models (Vaswani et al. 2017).\n\n2018\n\n•\n\nGPT, the first generative language model of OpenAI, a transformer of 120 million parameters\n(OpenAI 2018)\n\n2019\n\n•\n\nGPT-2, a transformer model of 1.5 billion parameters, impress the research community with its\nability to generate coherent texts (OpenAI 2019)\n\n2020\n\n•\n\nMarch - Deep neural networks can usually be adapted\nto be either discriminative or generative tasks, which has led to the development of various\ntypes of GenAI models, which can support different types of input and output data (modes).\n\nBackground and historical origins\nThis chapter provides a summary of the main technical principles around GenAI, including\nsome historical background. GenAI is currently defined more in a descriptive manner than\nby precise technical features. The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development\n(OECD) defines GenAI as \"a technology that can create content, including text, images, audio,\nor video, when prompted by a user\" (Lorenz et al. 2023). \"Prompts\" here correspond to textual\ninstructions, usually produced by the human users, optionally combined with some given\ndata. Although not mentioned, it is expected that the generated content is new, meaningful\nand human-like.\nIn the recent AI Act, the European Union defines GenAI as a type of foundation model (European\nCommission, European Parliament 2023). Foundation models correspond to general purpose\nAI models trained on large and diverse datasets in order to be used more easily for many\ndifferent tasks. GenAI systems are a specific subset of foundation models \"specifically intended\nto generate, with varying levels of autonomy, content such as complex text, images, audio\nor video.\" This definition emphasizes that new content is generated based on existing large\ntraining datasets, raising various issues and biases more particularly addressed by the AI Act.\nFrom the point of view of general users, one key aspect is that unlike the traditional \"supervised\"\nmachine learning models, which require a large amount of task-specific annotated training data,\nthese models can generate new content just by writing natural language prompts. Therefore,\nusing GenAI tools based on these models does not require technical skills. For the first time,\nmodern cutting-edge AI becomes directly accessible to the general public.\nThis accessibility has made possible a widespread diffusion of GenAI tools in the last two\nyears. For example, in 2022, models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al. 2021) and Midjourney\n(Midjourney 2022) have attracted a lot of attention on social media and democratized GenAI\nin popular culture (Midjourney 2022). ChatGPT for conversational systems became the fastest\nproduct to attain 100 million users (OpenAI 2022). On the professional side, GitHub Copilot\n(GitHub 2021) has anchored GenAI in software development: 92% of US-based developers are\nalready using AI coding tools according to a recent GitHub survey (GitHub 2023b).\nThe developments that led to GenAI have been a long and steady progress in the field of\nmachine learning and neural networks. Amari-Hopfield Network (Amari 1972, Hopfield 1982),\na type of neural network with associative memory, and Long Short Term Memory (LSTM)\nrecurrent neural networks (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber 1997), are often mentioned as early\nfoundations for the development of GenAI. The Amari-Hopfield Network demonstrated how\nnetworks could store and retrieve patterns, resembling human memory processes. LSTM\n\n\b19\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 22 / 116 =====\n\n20\b\n\nrecurrent neural networks expanded on this by introducing a mechanism to capture and learn\ncomplex sequential patterns, overcoming the limitations of traditional recurrent networks in\nhandling long-range dependencies.\nEarly effective GenAI was however not based on neural networks, but on probabilistic\ngraphical models such as Markov networks, which learn transitions over states in graph-based\nrepresentations rather than using bio-inspired structures. These statistical language models\nhad already led to practical commercial applications in the 1990s.\nLanguage models aim at predicting a next \"token,\" for example a word, given a sequence\nof observed tokens. Applied iteratively, it is possible to generate text or speech that mimics\nhuman language. This iterative method for generating sequences, like a sequence of words,\nis characteristic of so-called autoregressive models and can be viewed as an auto-completion\nfunction. Successful early applications include machine translation, such as Google statistical\nmachine translations deployed in the 2000s, as well as speech and text generation.\n\nDeep learning\nIn the 2010s, neural networks became the dominant approach in AI with deep learning.\nAlthough neural networks are well known since the 1950s (Rosenblatt 1957), these models\ncould only use a very limited number of neurons and layers – such as the so-called multilayer\nperceptron (MLP) – until the 1990s. Deep learning is the result of 30 years of cumulative\nprogress to increase (\"deepen\") the number of layers of neural networks.\nWith traditional machine learning techniques, the performance can quickly reach a plateau as\nthe amount of training data increases. Adding more data thus becomes useless after a while.\nOne of the key properties of deep learning is that the performance continuously increases with\nthe increase in training data. In other words, the more data we feed to a deep neural network\n(DNN), the better the deep neural network generally performs. The performance of these\nmodels becomes conditioned by the capacity of the computers and the amount of data used for\ntraining. Deep learning can surpass any other machine learning approaches, as long massive\ndata and computing resources are available.\nOne of the main findings of the WIPO Technology Trends on Artificial Intelligence was that deep\nlearning was by far the biggest and fastest growing technique in AI at the end of the 2010s,\nboth for patents and non-patent literature (WIPO 2019). The progress in deep learning led to\nbreakthrough results in so-called generative tasks.\n\nDiscriminative versus generative tasks\nDeep neural networks can usually be adapted to two different kinds of tasks:\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\n–\n\n–\n\nDiscriminative tasks involve a decision on the input data, such as classification, identifying\nnames in texts or segmenting an image. Discriminative models are models adapted and\ntrained to separate input data into these different classes.\nGenerative tasks involve the creation of new data samples given some input data. Generative\nmodels are models adapted and trained to create such new data. They are typically used to\ntranslate text, generate images, summarize text or answer questions.\n\nFigures 2 and 3 illustrate these fundamental types of machine learning tasks. Discriminative\nmodels excel in classification but cannot generate new data. In contrast, generative models\ncan also address discriminative tasks, but with lower accuracy than discriminative models.\nGenerative models have more parameters, are computationally more expensive and often\nrequire more training data than discriminative models.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 23 / 116 =====\n\nThe model must learn how to distinguish two classes: the painting Mona Lisa and\nother paintings. Among the\nmost significant are generative adversarial networks (GANs), decoder-based large language\nmodels (LLMs), variational autoencoders (VAE), and diffusion models.\n\nGenerative adversarial network\nA generative adversarial network (GAN) is a deep learning model for task generation\nintroduced in 2014 by Goodfellow et al. (2014). A GAN consists of two parts, a generator\nand a discriminator. The generator is a neural network generating output images and the\ndiscriminator a neural network evaluating how realistic the image generated by the generator\nis. The generation process is then a competition between these two parts. The generator\ntries to improve its output to mislead the discriminator and the discriminator tries to improve\nits ability to distinguish real images from generated ones, to avoid being misled by the\ngenerator. As a result, the generator will maximize its capacity to generate realistic images.\nGANs are used today for many tasks involving images such as generation and enhancement of\nphotorealistic images.\n\nLarge Language Models\nLarge Language Models (LLMs) are the basis of modern conversational systems (chatbots)\nsuch as ChatGPT or Bard. These models are trained on large datasets to learn the patterns\nand structures within the data, enabling them to generate new content that is coherent and\ncontextually relevant. LLMs in GenAI focus specifically on generating human-like text by\npredicting the next statistically most likely word and are used for various natural language\nprocessing tasks, including text completion, language translation, summarization and more.\nThe training process of LLMs involves pre-training on a large corpus of text data, allowing the\nmodel to learn the statistical properties and linguistic nuances of the language. To achieve this,\nmost of the LLMs use transformers, a type of neural network architecture designed specifically\nfor natural language processing (NLP) tasks, that were introduced first in 2017 (Vaswani et\nal. 2017). Transformers have allowed researchers to train increasingly large models without\nhaving to label all the data beforehand. They are based on the idea of self-attention, which\nmeans they can focus on different parts of the text simultaneously. This allows them to capture\nlong-range dependencies in text, which is important for understanding and phrasing complex\nlanguage. As a result, LLM-based chatbots are able to generate text that is coherent and\ncontextually relevant.\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\nOnce trained, the models can be fine-tuned for specific tasks or used directly to generate\ndiverse and contextually appropriate text. Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs)\nhave been gradually taking the lead from traditional LLMs. MLLMs are overcoming the limitation\nof purely text-based input and can access knowledge from multiple modalities – and can thus\ninteract more fully with the real world.\n\n(Variational) Auto-encoder\nAn auto-encoder model is based on three parts: encoder, code and decoder. The encoder is\na neural network that learns how to encode and compress input data into an intermediary\nrepresentation, the code, which is basically a sequence of numbers. The code is then used by\nthe decoder, another neural network, which has learnt how to decompress and reconstruct data\ninto the expected input format. Beyond data compression, the objective of the autoencoders is\nto learn how to represent the nature of some data, so that a small modification of this internal\nrepresentation can still be re-constructed into a new meaningful output. Autoencoders are\ncommon in GenAI today. A large number of variants have introduced multiple improvements,\nsuch as the popular variational autoencoders (VAEs), published in 2013 (Kingma and Welling\n2013), and used for generating sophisticated and diverse image samples.\nThe original transformer model (Vaswani et al. 2017) is also an encoder-decoder architecture. It\nhas been adapted for creating large language models used for text generation by keeping only\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 25 / 116 =====\n\nthe decoder part in the case of the OpenAI GPT model family. In other terms, modern LLMs are\ndecoder-based large language models.\n\n\b\n\n23\n\nAutoregressive models\nAutoregressive models are a class of probabilistic models that describe the probability\ndistribution of a sequence of observations by modeling the conditional probability of each\nobservation given the previous observations in the sequence. In other words, autoregressive\nmodels predict the next value in a sequence by considering the previous values.\nIn the context of GenAI, autoregressive models are often used to generate new data samples.\nA model is trained on a dataset, which is then used to generate new data points by predicting\none element at a time, based on the previously generated elements. This makes autoregressive\nmodels suitable for tasks such as language generation, image synthesis and other generative\ntasks. Examples of autoregressive models in GenAI include autoregressive moving average\n(ARMA) models, autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) models and PixelCNN\nfor image generation. Autoregressive models have been particularly successful when applied\nto natural language processing tasks (e.g. most modern LLMs, such as GPT-3 or GPT-4 are\nautoregressive) and image generation tasks, such as PixelCNN.\n\nDiffusion model\nDiffusion models are inspired from the concept of diffusion, which is used in physics to model\nthe movement of a set of particles in two different physical areas. However, there are also concerns about the increasing\nuse of GenAI models and tools, ranging from copyright infringement and the potential for\nmisuse, to the risk of job displacement.\n\nConcerns about the use of GenAI\nThe emergence of GenAI will have a significant impact on various industries, providing\ncompanies, organizations and individuals with unprecedented capabilities to create, synthesize\nand manipulate data. However, there are also concerns about the increasing use of GenAI\nmodels and tools, ranging from copyright infringement and the potential for misuse, to the risk\nof job displacement.\n\nThe impact of GenAI on the labor market\nIn terms of the impact on employment, a common fear is that GenAI will lead to significant job\nlosses in many industries as machines become capable of performing tasks previously done by\nhumans. A recent study by Goldman Sachs predicts that GenAI will significantly disrupt the labor\nmarket, with around 300 million workers in major global economies exposed to some degree\nof GenAI automation (Financial Times 2023). However, the impact will vary significantly from\njob to job. In fact, many professions will benefit from GenAI tools that complement their work\nand enhance their capabilities, allowing professionals to focus on higher-level, more strategic\naspects of their work. As a result, GenAI, like other forms of automation before it, should boost\nGDP growth and improve overall income levels, particularly for countries. However, some\njobs are certainly at risk of becoming obsolete, and AI-sensitive individuals will need targeted\nsupport to transition to new opportunities and retrain for emerging roles. Moreover, unlike\nprevious waves of automation, which mainly affected middle-skilled workers, the risks of AI\ndisplacement extend to certain higher-paid positions such as certain types of data analysts,\nmarket research analysts, bookkeepers or paralegals.\n\nThe role of GenAI regarding copyright and the protection of\nintellectual property\nConcerns have also been raised about potential copyright infringement of GenAI art, text and\ncode, as well as training data. AI models may generate text, images and audio that closely\nresemble existing works, potentially infringing copyright. As a result, copyright issues are\nalready fueling debate in a number of jurisdictions. In the US, for example, artists, writers\nand others have filed lawsuits accusing major AI companies such as OpenAI of using their\ncopyrighted work to train AI systems without permission (Brittain 2023). Another much debated\nissue is the question whether AI inventions can be patented, as AI models and tools play an\nincreasingly important role in innovation activities. For example, a recent decision by the US\nFederal Circuit stated that inventions developed purely by an AI machine are not patentable,\nwhereas inventions made by humans with the assistance of AI are (Nemec and Rann 2023).\n\n\b67\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 70 / 116 =====\n\n68\b\n\nThe examples above are non-exhaustive, and full consideration of the various legal issues\nsurrounding IP and GenAI are beyond the scope of this study.1\n\nOther concerns include deepfakes and training biases\nOther areas of concern include deepfakes, which are fake images or videos that insert a person’s\nlikeness into another video without their consent. Because GenAI can create highly realistic and\nconvincing content, these deepfakes can be used for malicious purposes, such as spreading\nmisinformation during election campaigns.\nIn addition, while the capabilities of GenAI models have improved significantly in recent years,\nthese models still sometimes produce incorrect results. For example, answers from chatbots\nmay sound convincing but may be wrong (e.g. AI hallucination)2 or biased due to distortions in\nthe training datasets. GenAI, and AI in general, is only as good as the data it is trained on, and\nexisting biases in the training data will lead to biases in the results. Therefore, it is important\nthat humans remain involved in decision-making, especially in industries where trust is\nparamount, such as finance or healthcare (Baxter and Schlesinger 2023).\n\nWill GenAI evolve into a general AI?\nAlthough the latest GenAI models use human-like language and appear creative and intelligent\nin their output, they are still far away from human intelligence, as GenAI models do not really\nunderstand things but instead are still only capable of making very good guesses based on\ntheir input data. Whether GenAI models can be further improved to have the ability to reason is\nthe subject of much debate. Some AI advocates believe that GenAI is an essential step towards\ngeneral AI and even consciousness. There are concerns that progress in the development of\nGenAI requires a sense of urgency to ensure that humanity can still contain and manage these\nmodels. Some experts have even called for a pause in AI development (Narayan et al. 2023).\nOthers argue that even faster advances in AI would provide tools to better understand the\ntechnology and make it safer. One example is OpenAI’s use of reinforcement learning from\nhuman feedback (RLHF) to create guardrails that make ChatGPT’s responses more accurate\nand appropriate (Goldman Sachs 2023). It is also important to note that there is significant\ndisagreement among experts about when general AI will be achieved, with many experts\nbelieving that general AI is still a long way off (World Economic Forum 2023).\n\nRegulations are being developed to address these concerns\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\nIn light of the above concerns about GenAI, new GenAI regulations are being developed and\nintroduced around the world with the aim of harnessing the benefits of GenAI while mitigating\nthe risks. The goals of regulation vary from country to country, but typically include protecting\nconsumers, preventing misuse and ensuring responsible development.\nChina was one of the first countries to introduce legislation on GenAI, just months after\nChatGPT was launched (Yang 2024). The country initially focused on individual pieces of\nlegislation for different types of AI products. As a result, in 2023 China had different rules\nfor algorithmic recommendation services than for deepfakes or GenAI. The AI Act\nwill regulate providers of artificial intelligence systems by categorizing the type of applications\ninto different risk categories, including a category for a potential future general AI. Some steps to\nachieve this include (Gillis 2023):\n–\n\n–\n–\n–\n–\n–\n\nTransparency and explanations: there should be documentation of the training data used\nand the algorithms employed to avoid potential copyright infringement and to allow users to\nunderstand how the GenAI models work. Patent classes are built after a\nconcept has reached a certain popularity, keywords are sharpened and defined only after they\nappear for the first time. While it has always been difficult to do traditional patent analysis on\nemerging topics, it is now closer to being impossible to get an accurate collection of patents\nusing traditional tools. Digital fields now follow different laws of development compared to\nclassical technical advancements. New concepts or technological improvements can spread\nrapidly due to the extensive reach of global networks. GenAI, such as ChatGPT, is an example of\nthis. Within just five days, the tool had a million users, while streaming services like Netflix took\n3.5 years to reach the same number of users. Even before the patent system can respond in\nthe form of new patent classification classes/subclasses, groups etc. or well-defined keywords,\ntechnology development has already advanced several steps further. At the same time, generic\nor descriptive terminology is used that is either nonspecific or semantically ambiguous. GenAI\nalso exemplifies this issue: there is a semantic difference between saying “an image generated\nby AI” (from data that did not previously exist) and “AI was used in the image generation\nprocess” (as in image classification or in object detection etc.).\n\n6 Further considerations\n\nPatent analysis is an established field and has been widely used for years, and while tools have\nbecome more powerful, the methodology has not changed dramatically. Analysts use patent\nclasses and keywords to build searches and count, measure and visualize the results.\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 72 / 116 =====\n\n70\b\n\nIt was already useful to use AI for the WIPO Technology Trends Report on AI (WIPO 2019), but\nit is even more relevant to use modern classification tools and advanced LLMs today to create\nan accurate patent collection for GenAI, as done in this report. We have already seen the\nintroduction of patent-trained LLMs, e.g. from Google with BERT for Patents (Google 2020), from\nEPO for automatic classification (EPO 2023) and for patent search with Searchformer (Vowinckel\nand Hähnke 2023) or from academia (Freunek and Bodmer 2021).\nHowever, the impact of GenAI on how people will generate text, visuals, insights, statistics and\neven new innovations using GenAI tools has the potential to turn the patent analytics space,\nlike many others, upside down. Instead of using patent classes that first need to be discussed,\ncreated and agreed, it will be possible to ask GenAI tools to show areas of development or to\ncollect the appropriate patents for new cutting-edge concepts directly on a prompt or with\nfine-tuned models. The patent analysis of GenAI, that we have conducted in this report, should\ntherefore be understood as an approach using the most advanced methods and tools available\nat the time of the analysis. It is to be expected that these tools will develop faster than the\ntraditional patent information system, and will therefore be able to challenge old patent analysis\nmethods very soon.\n\nWe are only seeing the beginning of the GenAI patent wave\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\nCollecting the patents for GenAI has been a challenge, but the growth is clearly visible and\nmeasurable. In contrast to modern LLMs that only retain the decoder part of the\noriginal transformer architecture, BERT only keeps the encoder part to address discriminative\ntasks. BERT is pretrained on large amounts of text data. The training involves two main\nprocesses: 1. Masked language modeling, and 2. Next sentence prediction.\nIn masked language modeling, words in the input text are randomly selected and replaced\nwith a special [MASK] token. The model is then trained to predict the original words based on\nthe context provided by the surrounding words. This helps learning the bidirectional context of\nwords in a sentence.\nIn next sentence prediction, the model is trained to predict for pairs of sentences whether the\nsecond sentence in a pair follows the first sentence. This task helps capturing relationships\nbetween sentences and understanding the overall context of the text input.\nIn order to use BERT in practical applications, it is fine-tuned to the specific task with labeled\ndata in a supervised manner. BERT has proven to be highly effective and is utilized in various\nnatural language processing tasks, including text classification, entity recognition, sentiment\nanalysis and more.\nThere are different variants of BERT available, such as BERT Base, BERT Large, cased, uncased\nand there are models that are based on BERT but differ from it in terms of parameters, training\nmethods, languages, such as RoBERTa, DistilBERT, XLM-RoBERTa etc. In this study, we use the\nBERT base uncased model with 110M parameters.\nSentence BERT\nSentence-BERT (SBERT) is a modification of the BERT model and is specifically designed for the\ncalculation of text representations (text embeddings or text vectors).\nThe key idea behind SBERT is to fine-tune BERT on datasets that are crafted for sentence-level\ntasks. The model learns to produce embeddings that capture semantic information about\nsentences. Text similarities can be calculated based on calculations of the cosine-similarity\nof the embeddings. There is a maximum context length, which is typically about 512 tokens,\nso SBERT is not restricted to sentences in the literal sense. There are different types of SBERT\nmodels with varying performance in semantic search, encoding speed and model size.\n\nPatent searches/prompts\n\nPatent Landscape Report – Generative Artificial Intelligence\n\nTo gather the basic dataset we have used three approaches:3\n–\n\n–\n\n–\n\n3\n\nMethod 1a: A patent search using generic terms such as “generative AI” to locate patents\nusing specific keyword concepts only. not formally cited like journal or\nconference articles. Citations to software and datasets are mostly informal mentions in the body\nof the text. As a consequence, they are invisible and unavailable in the large bibliographical and\ncitation databases like Web of Science or Scopus. To measure realistic impact of datasets and\nsoftware, it is necessary to identify their mentions with text mining techniques applied to the\nfull texts of NPL documents.\nThe corpus of scientific publications used in the study is a set of 75,870 deduplicated\npublications produced as explained in Appendix 6. We used the open access subset of this\ncorpus to identify automatically the mentions of software and datasets. These mentions can be\nthen exploited to estimate what are the most impactful software and datasets in GenAI.\nOur methodology relies on mature text mining tools resulting from several years of\ndevelopments. These techniques are well-evaluated and able to scale to thousands of\ndocuments at a reasonable cost. The process is as follows:\n–\n\n–\n\n–\n–\n\nDocument full text acquisition: The 75,870 publications of our corpus include 48,784 Open\nAccess publications according to lens.org. We successfully downloaded 34,183 PDFs out of\nthe full open access subset using the Unpaywall database (https://unpaywall.org).\nText mining of mentions: The PDFs were processed by two tools, Softcite for extracting the\nsoftware mentions (Softcite (2018–2024)) and DataStet for extracting dataset mentions\nDataStet (2022–2024).\nAggregation: The extracted mentions (789,218 software mentions and 978,297 dataset\nmentions) were then aggregated and we kept the top 500 datasets and top 500 software.\nData cleaning: We manually corrected these two sets to remove errors and merge the same\nsoftware mentioned with name variants not handled at aggregation time.\n\nThe two mentioned recognizers rely on deep learning techniques, a BERT model called SciBERT,\ntrained on scientific content. Softcite was trained on 5,000 manually annotated scientific articles\nand DataStet on two sets of respectively 22,000 and 6,000 manually annotated sentences. They\nperform with high accuracy, around 81% F1-score for software names and 89% F1-score for\ndataset names. More details on the implementation, evaluation and application of these tools\nare available in Lopez et al. (2021) and in Bassinet et al. (2023).\nWhile the mentions were extracted from documents corresponding to less than half of the\nwhole GenAI corpus, this is a very large and representative subset. Open Access publications are\ndominantly from scientific publishers (gold open access) and are not associated to loss of quality\n(STM 2023). Therefore, we can consider that the derived statistics provide a good approximation\nof the dataset and software relative impact.\n\nA.8 Descriptions/example patents for GenAI applications\nSoftware/other applications\n\nAppendices\n\nMany GenAI patent families cannot be assigned to a specific application based on patent title,\nabstract and claims.
