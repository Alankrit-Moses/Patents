It is used in many applications in\nthe form of dioxide, elementary metal or in alloys. Titanium is mainly extracted from ilmenite ore\n(82 percent), other sources being slags (13 percent) and rutile ore (5 percent). About 94 percent\nof the world’s titanium is used to produce titanium dioxide, with the remaining 6 percent being\nused for titanium metal and alloys. China is the largest producer of titanium dioxide and titanium\nmetal, followed by the United States of America (US) for titanium dioxide and Japan for titanium\nmetal. This report provides a landscape of patenting activity relating to the production of\ntitanium from ilmenite from 2002 to 2022, along with a section on patenting activity for selected\nindustrial applications of titanium and titanium dioxide.\n\nPatenting activity related to titanium dioxide production from ilmenite\nis rapidly increasing\nBetween 2002 and 2022, there have been 459 patent families that describe the production of\ntitanium dioxide from ilmenite, and this number is growing rapidly. The majority of these patents\ndescribe pre-treatment processes, such as using smelting and magnetic separation to increase\ntitanium concentration in low-grade ores, leading to titanium concentrates or slags. Other patents\ndescribe processes to obtain titanium dioxide, either by a direct hydrometallurgical process or\nthrough two industrially exploited processes, the sulfate process and the chloride process.\nAcid leaching might be used either as a pre-treatment or as part of a hydrometallurgical process\nto directly obtain titanium dioxide or synthetic rutile (>90 percent titanium dioxide, TiO2). The\nsulfate process represents 40 percent of the world’s titanium dioxide production and is protected\nin 23 percent of patent families. The chloride process is only mentioned in 8 percent of patent\nfamilies, although it provides 60 percent of the worldwide industrial production of titanium\ndioxide. Given the findings from this patent landscape report, we can expect to see industrial\nchloride process use progressively decrease within the next few years and an increasing use of\ndirect hydrometallurgical processes, which require fewer steps and are more convenient.\nKey contributors to patents on the production of titanium dioxide are companies from China,\nAustralia and the United States, reflecting the major contribution of these countries to industrial\nproduction. Chinese companies Pangang and Lomon Billions Groups are the main contributors\nand hold diversified patent portfolios covering both pre-treatment and the processes leading to a\nfinal product.\n\nPatenting activity related to titanium metal production from ilmenite\nremains stable\nBetween 2002 and 2022, there have been 92 patent families that describe the production\nof titanium metal from ilmenite, and this number has remained quite steady. These patents\ndescribe the production of titanium metal starting from mineral ores, such as ilmenite,\nand from titanium dioxide (TiO2) and titanium tetrachloride (TiCl4), a chemical obtained as\nan intermediate in the chloride process. The starting materials are purified if needed, and\nthen converted to titanium metal by a chemical reduction process using a reducing agent.\nProcesses mainly differ in regard to the reducing agent used to transform the starting\nmaterial into titanium metal: magnesium is the most frequently cited reducing agent and the\nmost exploited in industrial production.\n\n﻿\n\nKey players in the field are Japanese companies, in particular Toho Titanium and Osaka Titanium\nTechnologies, both focusing on reduction using magnesium. Pangang also contributes to\ntitanium metal production and holds patents describing reduction by molten salt electrolysis.\n5\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 7 / 56 =====\n\nAs the patent dynamics are slow in this field and most industrial players describe the standard\nindustrial process, we do not expect a big shift from this process within the next few years.\nThe one emerging technology seems to be molten salt electrolysis developed by Pangang.\n\nTitanium dioxide and titanium metal are used in a wide range of\nindustrial applications\nPatents on industrial applications of titanium dioxide and titanium from 2012 to 2022 were\nanalyzed, focusing on six technological domains: ceramics, electrodes for batteries, medical\ntechnology, cosmetics, coatings and water treatment.\n\nPatent Landscape Report: Ilmenite\n\n• 2,027 patent families described the increasing use of titanium dioxide and titanium\ncomposites in ceramics. It is used in several applications, mainly in the form of\ntitanium dioxide, but also as titanium metal, or in alloys. Titanium dioxide is present worldwide in\ncoastal deposits of heavy-mineral sands, which are sources of heavy industrial minerals, such as\nilmenite, rutile and leucoxene.\nThis report provides a landscape of the patent activity related to the process of extracting titanium\ndioxide or titanium metal from ilmenite ore. In addition, a section on the industrial applications\nof titanium dioxide and titanium metal is included, focusing on selected applications, such as\nceramics, electrodes for batteries, medical technology, cosmetics, coatings and water treatment.\nThe mineral ilmenite is mainly composed of iron and titanium oxide having an ideal chemical\nformula FeTiO3 and a stoichiometric content of 53 percent titanium dioxide (TiO2), although the\nreal TiO2 content varies between 40 and 65 percent (Van Gosen and Ellefsen, 2018; Ngugen and\nLee, 2019). Indeed, weathering can increase the TiO2 content in titaniferous ores by leaching the\niron. Natural rutile contains around 95 percent TiO2, while leucoxene comprises a mixture of\nFeTiO3 and TiO2 with a total TiO2 content over 65 percent (Ngugen and Lee, 2019).\nIlmenite is commonly processed to obtain a titanium concentrate, which is called “synthetic\nrutile” if it contains more than 90 percent TiO2, or more generally “titaniferous slags” if it has a\nlower TiO2 content.\nMore than 80 percent of the estimated global production of titanium concentrate is obtained\nfrom the processing of ilmenite, while 13 percent is obtained from titaniferous slags and\n5 percent from rutile (Figure 1).\nFigure 1. About 94 percent of\nthe world’s titanium consumption in 2020 was used to produce titanium dioxide (USGS, n.d.).\nIlmenite is the most abundant titanium-containing mineral but has a low titanium dioxide content\nand contains iron along with impurities such as dioxides of chromium, manganese, vanadium,\nmagnesium, aluminum, calcium and silicon (Gázquez et al., 2014).\nSand ilmenite is often processed by smelting or redox pre-treatments to increase its titanium\ncontent, leading to “titanium concentrate” or “upgraded slags” (Gázquez et al., 2014). Upgraded\nslags are commonly treated with the sulfate process to obtain titanium dioxide (Figure 6). This\nprocess represented around 40 percent of the total TiO2 production in 2014 (Gázquez et al., 2014).\nOther processes, such as the Becher process, have been used to directly transform ilmenite into\nsynthetic rutile, which has a TiO2 content of over 90 percent and can be treated with the chloride\nprocess to obtain titanium dioxide as the final product (see Figure 6). Overall, the chloride process\nrepresented around 60 percent of the world’s TiO2 production in 2014 (Gázquez et al., 2014).\nFigure 6. Different processes leading to titanium dioxide from ilmenite ore.\nExtraction of titanium dioxide from ilmenite ore is commonly achieved by the sulfate and chloride\nprocesses after smelting or redox pre-treatments. Recently, some\nhydrometallurgical processes have emerged that directly obtain titanium dioxide from ilmenite\nsands or rocks. Hydrometallurgical processes consist of multiple steps, and usually fall into\none of two categories: (1) smelting followed by acid leaching at high temperature; (2) redox\npre-treatments or roasting followed by acid and/or alkaline leaching (Ngugen and Lee, 2019). A\nmagnetic separation step can be included to increase titanium content.\n\nThe sulfate process\n\nThe sulfate process represented about 40 percent of the total TiO2 production in 2014 (Gázquez\net al., 2014). In this process, ilmenite is reacted with concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4). The\nresulting solution contains titanyl sulfate (TiOSO4) and iron sulfate (FeSO4) dissolved in sulfuric\nacid. A chemical reduction process is required to ensure that all iron is dissolved. The solution\nis concentrated, titanium sulfate hydrolyzed and hydrated titanium dioxide precipitated and\nseparated by filtration. The precipitate is calcined, leading to the growth of TiO2 crystals and\nremoval of residual water and acid (Gázquez et al., 2014).\nThe general reaction is:\nFeTiO3 + 2H2SO4 → TiOSO4 + FeSO4 + H2O [dissolution]\nTiOSO4 + H2O → TiO2 nH2O + H2SO4 [TiO2 precipitation]\nTiO2 nH2O → TiO2 + nH2O [TiO2 calcination]\nThe sulfate process is simple and cheap, can be applied to low-grade ores, and produces a\npigment suitable for use in papers, ceramics and inks (Gázquez et al., 2014). However, the quality\nof product is low and large quantities of waste iron sulfate produced. The overall yield is about\n0.5 tonnes of titanium dioxide for approximately 1 tonnes of starting raw ilmenite material\n(Gázquez et al., 2014).\n\nThe chloride process\n\nThe chloride process represented nearly 60 percent of total TiO2 production in 2014 (Gázquez\net al., 2014). It can be used for ilmenite, slag or rutile, but commonly natural or synthetic rutile is\nused, which limits iron waste by-product.\nIn this process, the raw material is mixed with gaseous chlorine in the presence of coke as a\nreducing agent. The resulting gas stream contains titanium tetrachloride (TiCl4), carbon dioxides\nand chlorides of metal impurities (except for silica and zirconium, which are not chlorinated and\nremain in the reactor). The gas stream is cooled with liquid TiCl4 at a temperature where the\nother metal chlorides solidify and are separated. The purified TiCl4 stream is further cooled and\ncondensed as liquid, then reacted with oxygen at high temperature to form titanium dioxide and\nchlorine, which is recycled. Residual chlorine is removed by hydrolysis (Gázquez et al., 2014).\nThe general reaction process is:\n2TiO2 + 3C + 4Cl2 → 2TiCl4 + 2CO + CO2 [chlorination]\nTiCl4 (gas, impure) → TiCl4 (pure, liquid) [purification]\n\nPatent Landscape Report: Ilmenite\n\nTiCl4 + O2 → TiO2 + 2Cl2\n\n12\n\nThe waste produced is mainly coke and unchlorinated solid impurities, along with an acid solution\nfollowing addition of HCl to the residues of chlorination (Gázquez et al., 2014). Consumption of\nchloride depends on the amount of iron present in the raw material and hence this process is\nused only with raw material of a high titanium content.\n\nThe Becher process\n\nIlmenite ores can be upgraded to synthetic rutile by increasing their TiO2 content to between 90\nand 96 percent. In the Becher process, a chemical reduction is applied to iron in the presence of\ncoke, and the material is then leached by ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) to oxidize and precipitate\nthe iron as dioxide or hydroxide as fine particles, which are then separated from the larger\nparticles of TiO2 (Gázquez et al., 2014).\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 14 / 56 =====\n\nPatent landscape\nPatent activity\n\nAmong the 2,235 patent families obtained from the search, 459 related to the production of\ntitanium dioxide from ilmenite and were considered for in-depth analysis. It has low density, good strength, excellent corrosion resistance to\nsalt and acids, and low electric and thermal conductivities (El Khalloufi et al., 2021).\nTitanium metal is used in many different applications owing to its special properties. The most\ncommon titanium alloy is Ti6A-4V (90 percent titanium, 6 percent aluminum, 4 percent vanadium)\nand typically used in medical implants (El Khalloufi et al., 2021).\nThe production of titanium metal represents only a small fraction (6 percent in 2020) of total\ntitanium consumption, with the remainder being industrially exploited as titanium dioxide.\nTitanium metal is commonly obtained in the form a sponge, which can be reprocessed to obtain\ncompact metal, such as ingots or billets, or a fine powder (Figure 15).\nFigure 15. Different forms of titanium.\nTitanium sponge is the most common form of the metal, which can be processed to obtain different\nproducts such as rods, ingots, and titanium metal powder.\n\nSource: (left-hand image) https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Titanium_sponge_cylinder,_120_grams.jpg\n(right-hand image) adapted from https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Titanium_products.jpg\n\nProcesses\n\nFor the production of titanium metal, titanium dioxide (TiO2) or titanium tetrachloride (TiCl4)\nderived from the chloride process are typically used. The standard industrially used process is the\nKroll process first developed by Du Pont Germany in 1848 (El Khalloufi et al., 2021). The Hunter and\nArmstrong processes are also commercially used, but not considered economically competitive (El\nKhalloufi et al., 2021).\n\nThe Kroll process\n\nThe Kroll process uses TiCl4 produced by the chloride process as the starting material (El Khalloufi\net al., 2021). Titanium is reduced by the addition of metallic magnesium (Mg). Mg and magnesium\nchloride (MgCl2) are then separated by vacuum distillation or helium sweeping followed by\nleaching. Mg is then recycled by electrolysis of MgCl2. A sponge of titanium metal is obtained as\nthe final product:\n2TiCl4 (liquid) + 2 Mg (solid) → Ti (solid) + 2 MgCl2 (liquid) [reduction]\nMgCl2 (liquid) → Mg (solid) + Cl2 (gas) [recycling by electrolysis]\n20\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 22 / 56 =====\n\nThe Hunter and Armstrong processes\n\nThe Hunter and Armstrong processes are similar to the Kroll process, but sodium (Na) is used\nto reduce titanium instead of magnesium (El Khalloufi et al., 2021). These processes are more\nexpensive, because four molecules of sodium are required to reduce one molecule of titanium\ntetrachloride or titanium dioxide, whereas only two molecules of magnesium are required for\nthe same purpose. Recovering sodium by electrolysis is as expensive as recovering magnesium,\nand this makes the process using sodium more expensive, although the purity of the obtained\ntitanium powder is higher (99 percent) (El Khalloufi et al., 2021).\n\nPatent landscape\nPatent activity\n\nAmong the 304 patent families found, 92 related to the production of metal titanium and were\nconsidered for in-depth analysis. For the rest, titanium dioxide is used in the chemical industry for the manufacturing\nof carbides and other compounds.\nTitanium metal and alloys are used for the following:\n• the production of spacecraft and missiles in the aerospace and military industries, because of\ntheir strength-to-weight ratio;\n• coatings for ships and seawater-cooled power plants, because of their high resistance to\nsalt corrosion;\n• a catalyst in petroleum refineries and in the chemical industry;\n• the production of artificial joints, stents and surgical instruments in the medical industry;\n• lightweight consumer goods and sports equipment, such as jewelry, golf clubs and bicycles\n(El Khalloufi et al., 2021; Ngugen and Lee, 2019).\n\nPatent landscape\nThe patent landscape for the industrial applications of titanium dioxide and titanium metal is very\nlarge, reflecting the broad use of these compounds in very different fields. It also includes a section\non selected industrial applications of titanium-based compounds.\nSince 2002, patent activity with regard to the process of obtaining titanium dioxide from ilmenite\nhas grown strongly. Key contributors to patents on the production of titanium dioxide are\ncompanies from China, Australia and the United States, reflecting the major contribution of these\ncountries to the industrial production of titanium dioxide.\nSome patents describe pre-treatment processes to increase titanium concentration in low-grade\nores and obtain titanium concentrates or slags. Other patents describe processes to obtain\ntitanium dioxide, either by a direct hydrometallurgical process or through two industrially\nexploited processes, the sulfate and chloride processes.\nThe chloride process is described in very few patents, although it remains the most exploited\nprocess in the industrial production of titanium dioxide today. Given the findings in this patent\nlandscape, we can expect the chloride process to be progressively substituted by more direct\nhydrometallurgical processes in future, which require fewer steps and are more convenient.\nIn contrast, patent activity related to the process for the production of titanium metal has\nremained steady since 2002. Key players in this field are mainly Japanese companies. All industrial\nkey players describe the industrially exploited Kroll process, which is based on the reduction of\ntitanium dioxide or titanium tetrachloride with magnesium.\nGiven the slow patent dynamic and the fact that a large majority of patents describe the\nKroll process, we do not expect a big shift away from this process over the coming years. The\nonly emerging technology seems to be molten salt electrolysis developed by the Chinese\ncompany Pangang.\nA large and increasing number of patents filed since 2012 describe the use of titanium dioxide\nin ceramic insulators in electronics and as electrodes in batteries for electronic devices and\nautomobiles. Key players in these fields are several multinational corporations in electronics and\nautomobiles from Japan, the Republic of Korea and several Chinese universities. The presence\nof many leading industrial actors reflects the maturity of these technologies and their extended\nindustrial use.\nTitanium metal and titanium dioxide are also widely used in medical technology, and patent\nactivity in this field is increasing strongly. Patents describe implantable medical devices and\nsurgical instruments made of titanium metal or alloys, and their coatings made of titanium\ndioxide. This technological field is dominated by Chinese companies and universities.\nTitanium dioxide is also widely used in cosmetic compositions for skincare, make-up and oral\ncare. Key players in this field are almost exclusively companies coming from several countries\nin Asia, Europe and the United States. The significant presence of big companies among the top\nactors reflects the high maturity of this technology.\nOther emerging applications of titanium dioxide are in the fields of coatings and water treatment.\nTitanium-based coatings mainly prevent the corrosion of metal. Key players in this field are\ncompanies and universities from China.\nPatents in the field of water treatment describe the use of titanium dioxide or titanium composites\nas electrodes, photocatalysts, coagulants or in purification by UV irradiation, where titanium\ndioxide is used as a photocatalyst. This field is dominated by Chinese universities and the lack of\nindustrial contributors reflects the low maturity of this technology and its growth potential.\n\n47\n\n\n\n===== PAGE 49 / 56 =====\n\nAnnex\nMethodology and search strategy\nProduction of titanium dioxide\nScope definition\n\nThis section focuses on patents describing processes to obtain titanium dioxide from ilmenite\nore.
